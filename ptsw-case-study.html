Research & Analysis | IA | Copywriting | UI

Website redesign, narrative development, and event display materials for a growing non-profit woodworking school.

Overview

The Woodworking School started out very much as a bootstrap operation with borrowed tools, a makeshift space, and volunteer labor. Over the years as the school grew considerably, it was time to rethink the content, branding, and structure of the online and printed materials. I worked with instructors, staff, and board members to more accurately communicate the school’s values and address the student’s needs.

Working with a mission-based organization meant that the community we served needed to be at the core of everything we did. Understanding who our students were and what they were getting from their experience informed almost everything we developed for these projects. More so than pushing our branding to reflect romanticized visions of old-timey woodworking, we wanted to reflect the personal growth that many of our students felt from their time at the school.

Research & Discovery

Learning about the students

There were abundant opportunities to talk to prospective students, current students, alum, and casual visitors.

Talking to Visitors

Because the school was at a state park, a significant amount of foot traffic came through to see the school. Talking to visitors and potential students helped us pinpoint common questions and initial impressions.

2-hour Feedback Sessions

Feedback sessions at the end of our 12-week classes was invaluable for understanding what students were taking away from their experience vs. their expectations going into the course. Using their concepts and emotional reactions helped us write copy that reflected their experience and develop an authentic narrative.

Alumni Survey and Exit Surveys

Asking alum about their experience in an anonymous context helped us discover some issues that people were uncomfortable bringing up face to face.

Website Redesign

Restructuring the Information Architecture (IA)

Since the school had grown from offering about 20 classes/year to around 55, I wanted to restructure the course offerings from a spreadsheet format into a catalog format. This was divided by category and searchable by level, course-length/cost, and instructor. There was still a high demand for a calendar format, so we included a chronological version as well.

Browsing

I added short descriptions and images so students could browse offerings more easily. Links to the full class descriptions gave very detailed (and long) descriptions of curriculum and important information that were previously overwhelming to users.

Instructor Pages

For students who were searching for classes by instructor, I was able to list class offering links on instructorpages and syndicate those links so they only needed to be updated in one place. This both simplified the user flow for students and improved site consistiency.

Getting Here

Since navigating the physical space was a problem for students, I dedicated significant real estate to helping people get here and highlighting the location as a strength instead of an obstacle.

Long pages

I added section sub-menus to longer pages so it was easier to understand the structure of the page and find the information.

Visual Design

Logo

A logo redesign wasn’t in the budget, so instead we shifted to using the existing letterhead more frequently instead of the logo, which was somewhat outdated.

Colors and Type

I shifted the colors from green and khaki to blue-gray, ochre-yellow, and rusty red. I chose Palatino Linotype as our main typeface to convey a more traditional feel, but mixed in Oswald to give it a touch of modernity.

Platform

The platform we chose needed to be able to be maintained with volunteer or non-expert resources, so free tech support and an automatically responsive site were crucial.

vs

I chose Squarespace for the school because it would be sustainable for a low budget through potentially lean years and the office staff felt less intimidated by Squarespace than Wordpress.

    Challenges

  • Working with templates limited the ability to customize the design.

  • Squarespace isn't primairly designed to support a website this large. (WP would have been better)

  • No archive of pages, anything accidentally deleted is gone forever.

Developing the Narrative

Centering the Message

"We're committed to passing on the living tradition of woodworking--rooted in techniques that create lasting results and instilling the values of fine craftsmanship, sustainability, and creative expression."

Website Homepage

The homepage went through a series of iterations. Featuring specific classes on the homepage was wholly ineffective as was listing upcoming classes.

Over time, I shifted the content to emphasize what makes the school distinct and reflect the mission.

Ultimately I simplified the homepage to include a welcome video, a newsletter signup, a space to feature a link to any events or news that had been recently promoted through marketing, and recent blog posts.

I wasn't expecting visitors to spend much time interacting with the homepage below the fold, and analytics reflected that most visitors go directly to the catalog or the calendar.

"Learn With Your Hands"

Event Presentation Materials

A significant amount of students came to the school after finding it at an event. We wanted update the presentation materials to support those interactions with visuals.

This was the easiest part because so much of the groundwork had already been done with the website and narrative.

Practical considerations were that any display needed to be collapsable, lightweight, easy to transport, and weatherproof. It was important that the materials didn’t feel too corporate or plasticy.

We decided to make hanging banners with turned wooden dowel supports to give them a handmade touch.
