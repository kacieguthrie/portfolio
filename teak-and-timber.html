Teak & Timber

Teak & Timber is a furniture company that sources premium-quality solid-wood furniture from local woodworkers. We built a project management tool for our gig-based ecommerce platform.

Problem

Independent woodworkers often have the skills and desire to build high-quality furniture, but lack the time, skills, and resources to market their services and manage their business. Teak and Timber aims to make handmade furniture more accessible and bring job offers to woodworkers.

Solution

Build project management portal that would allow woodworkers to accept, manage, and get paid for projects online while helping administrators keep an eye on their progress and address potential jam ups. For the first version, we were not accepting custom orders; only items from our catalog of established designs.

prototype
Research & Discovery

While I was learning about woodworker’s motivations, goals, and frustrations, I developed protopersonas -- theoretical personas based partially on interviews, partially on personal experience, and partially on our best guesses. Everyone on our team was an experienced woodworker, but we wanted to make sure we were getting a range of perspectives. These interviews validated some of our assumptions and gave us new insights.

Insights and Validations:

  • Most woodworkers were doing this part-time, so most of them didn’t have a dedicated office and in some cases didn’t have a formalized business process. Each project was handled ad hoc.

  • Their goals were organization and communication - some estimated they spent up to 20% of their time going back and forth with the client.

  • Finding woodworking commissions could be feast or famine, so it was important to be flexible in the amount of work they were willing/able to take on.

Revised Process Flow

As we learned more about what we needed to build, the original process changed, too. We learned that it wasn’t that important for customers to be able to pick their woodworker (an element that had previously required us to build our own ecommerce portal from scratch). We re-centered our efforts with a shared understanding of all the pieces that needed to be built.

This was an important learning point for us. In hindsight, the whole reason we had to build the ecommerce platform from scratch was based on a hypothesis that we hadn’t validated with research.

Sketches and Wireframes

Sketches

Quality assessment was a big question for our business model. After revisiting the process for managing a job, I realized this needed to be a checklist of items that woodworkers needed to report to us as they progressed in their projects.

Mockups

I wasn’t planning on making hi-fi mockups for this part. Branding wasn't our highest priority, so we planed to use Material UI since it works well with React and we could move pretty quickly. I ended up making the mockups because we had some interns working with us who needed a more solid “source of truth” for what the UI should look like. It also allowed me to do some remote testing before we started building

We decided to focus on the mobile version --the desktop version was a slightly larger version of the mobile app with the menu as a sidebar. Most folks have a mobile device these days, but not all of our woodworkers would have a desktop in their shop because of the dust. We knew parts of our process would require them to upload photos, so we encouraged them to use it on a mobile device.

Early Testing

gave some insights into navigation issues. Even though every page was accessible and the browser app allowed users to utilize the back button, it was better to have a back button built into the interface.
